Uncertainty-Aware, Structure-Preserving Machine Learning Approach For Domain Shift Detection From Nonlinear Dynamic Responses Of Structural Systems

Authors: David A. Najera-Flores, Justin Jacobs, D. Dane Quinn, Anthony Garland, Michael D. Todd

Publication Date: 2025-03-01

File: processed_papers/2024-NajeraFloresDA-RISK-a/2024-NajeraFloresDA-RISK-a.md

Paragraph Summary

"The paper presents a novel approach to structural health monitoring (SHM) by integrating an uncertainty-aware machine learning (ML) framework with a focus on structure-preserving characteristics. It addresses the challenge of detecting domain shifts\u2014situations where the data from an operational system diverges from the data used for training due to underlying structural changes, such as damage. The proposed method employs a spectral-normalized neural Gaussian process (SNGP) algorithm that combines neural networks' flexibility with Gaussian processes\u2019 uncertainty quantification capabilities. The model is designed to maintain distance preservation within the input state space, allowing it to effectively identify and respond to statistically significant shifts in the structure's dynamic responses, which are not directly observable due to spatial localization of the nonlinearities.\n\nThe authors demonstrate this approach by applying it to a computational example of a nonlinear structural system subjected to various damage conditions. The effectiveness of the methodology in detecting domain shifts is confirmed through systematic binary hypothesis testing on the variance of model predictions, showcasing its robustness even under normative changes. This study contributes to the advancement of digital twin technologies in SHM, facilitating real-time monitoring and predictive analytics in engineering applications, thereby ensuring reliability and safety in complex structural systems. Future work will explore the incorporation of additional uncertainties and the application of the proposed methods to broader contexts within structural engineering."

Key Points

  • The primary research question is to develop a machine learning approach for domain shift detection in structural health monitoring systems using a structure-preserving model with uncertainty awareness.
  • The main method involves integrating the spectral-normalized neural Gaussian process algorithm with a structure-preserving neural network framework to detect domain shifts due to structural changes.
  • The key finding is that the proposed model can effectively detect domain shifts and is robust to changes in normative operating conditions by leveraging predicted variances.
  • The significance of these results is the potential to enhance the reliability of structural health monitoring systems in aerospace, civil, or mechanical applications under varying conditions.
  • The novel contribution includes the extension of the spectral-normalized neural GP algorithm to a structure-preserving model, enabling uncertainty-aware detection based on predictive variance.
  • A major limitation is that the model's robustness to measurement noise and its applicability to other types of structures beyond the examples given are not fully explored yet.
  • Suggestions for future research include exploring applications to other structural systems, incorporating measurement noise, and integrating domain shift detection into cost and risk-informed SHM frameworks.

A Reduced Order Model Conditioned on Monitoring Features for Estimation and Uncertainty Quantification in Engineered Systems

Authors: Konstantinos Vlachas, Thomas Simpson, PhD, Anthony Garland, PhD, D. Dane Quinn, PhD, Charbel Farhat, PhD, Eleni Chatzi, PhD

Publication Date: 2024-07-10

File: processed_papers/2024-VlachasK-MSSP-a/2024-VlachasK-MSSP-a.md

Paragraph Summary

"This paper presents a novel Reduced Order Model (ROM) framework, termed *VpROM*, leveraging conditional Variational Autoencoders (cVAEs) to effectively predict responses in nonlinear parametric systems while integrating uncertainty quantification. Unlike traditional ROM methods that rely on predefined and often unknown physics-based parameters, the *VpROM* adaptively conditions its model on features derived from real-time monitoring data, allowing for a more practical and responsive model tuning in structural health monitoring (SHM). The framework combines a projection-based approach with generative deep learning to achieve robust local projection bases, thus maintaining high fidelity in system response predictions while simultaneously providing uncertainty estimates for these predictions.\n\nThe authors validate their approach through two distinct case studies: a two-dimensional arc structure experiencing geometric nonlinearities and a large-scale kingpin connection model characterized by plasticity. The results demonstrate that the *VpROM* effectively captures the dynamics and uncertainties inherent in the systems under consideration, achieving significant speed improvements\u2014up to 25 times faster than full-order model simulations\u2014while ensuring the delivery of reliable response envelopes encapsulating the uncertainty of the predictions. This framework effectively addresses the challenges of high computational costs in engineering simulations, particularly in applications involving SHM, and highlights the potential of integrating machine learning techniques with traditional modeling approaches in engineering disciplines."

Key Points

  • The paper focuses on developing a Reduced Order Model (ROM) that conditions parametric reductions on monitoring features obtained from sensor data.
  • A physics-based generative Reduced Order Model is introduced, which utilizes conditional Variational Autoencoders (cVAEs) to account for non-linear systems with parametric dependencies, enabling uncertainty quantification.
  • The cVAE is trained to map limited output measurements to model parameters, allowing the ROM to estimate quantities probabilistically.
  • The proposed method mitigates the limitation of typical ROMs that rely on predefined physical parameters by conditioning on easily accessible monitoring data.
  • Key challenges include the scalability of purely physics-based techniques and the accurate estimation of model parameters from limited data.
  • The paper proposes improvements in the training process to enhance robustness and generalization, incorporating variational loss terms for better accuracy.
  • Future work involves expanding this framework to handle systems with discontinuities or complex bifurcations, as current evaluations focus on smooth parametric variations.

Paragraph Summary

"The paper by Najera-Flores et al. investigates the impact of measurement noise on a machine learning framework for identifying isolated nonlinearities in structural systems. Previous work established a physics-informed neural network capable of estimating deviatoric forces from clean data, but real-world applications often involve noisy measurements. This study explores the sensitivity of the trained neural network to both noise-free and noisy data, employing conformal prediction methods to quantify uncertainty in response predictions. By leveraging statistical techniques such as the jackknife estimator, the authors define distribution-free prediction intervals for the neural network outputs, allowing the framework to be utilized effectively in structural health monitoring applications.\n\nIn their approach, the authors implement a structure-preserving machine learning model that captures the dynamics of systems with localized nonlinearities and employs conformal regression to handle uncertainty in predictions. The results demonstrate that the model's performance remains robust even with the introduction of noise, successfully identifying deviations in structural responses attributable to damage. The authors validate their framework using numerical examples and hypothesis testing to illustrate its effectiveness. Ultimately, the paper highlights the framework's potential for real-time application in structural health monitoring, identifying changes in system behavior while adequately quantifying uncertainty. Future work will focus on integrating broader sources of uncertainty and applying this methodology to experimental systems."

Key Points

  • The primary research objective is to quantify the uncertainty in a machine learning model's ability to identify isolated nonlinearities in structural systems using conformal prediction techniques.
  • The authors employ a physics-informed structure-preserving neural network method to model deviatoric forces at the boundaries of isolated nonlinear regions.
  • Key findings indicate that neural networks can effectively predict structural dynamics even when trained on noisy data, maintaining robustness through regularization effects inherent in the structure.
  • The results highlight the significant potential for improved structural health monitoring systems that can operate in noisy environments, leading to more reliable diagnostics and early detection of structural damage.
  • A novel contribution is the application of conformal prediction to establish prediction intervals for neural network outputs, providing a method for uncertainty quantification without requiring special network architecture modifications.
  • Major limitations include the assumed accessibility of response data at the boundaries of nonlinear regions and the challenges associated with noise propagation.
  • Future research is suggested to explore other types of uncertainties, like epistemic uncertainty, and to apply this methodology to real-world experimental data.

A structure-preserving machine learning framework for accurate prediction of structural dynamics for systems with isolated nonlinearities

Authors: David A. Najera-Flores, D. Dane Quinn, Anthony Garland, Konstantinos Vlachas, Eleni Chatzi, Michael D. Todd

Publication Date: 2024-03-15

File: processed_papers/Najera_isolated-1-s2.0-S0888327024002383-main/Najera_isolated-1-s2.0-S0888327024002383-main.md

Paragraph Summary

"This paper presents a novel machine learning framework aimed at accurately predicting the dynamics of structural systems that contain localized nonlinearities, such as those found in joints or interfaces. These nonlinearities complicate the development of effective reduced-order models due to their global impact on system dynamics. The authors introduce a structure-preserving approach that leverages a multilayer perceptron to predict a deviatoric force, which encapsulates the effects of these nonlinearities, based solely on measured responses at the boundaries of the nonlinear regions. By removing the need for direct numerical simulations of the nonlinearities, this method allows for more efficient modeling while maintaining the integrity of the underlying linear dynamics of the external structure.\n\nUtilizing a specific case involving cubic nonlinearities and hysteretic damping within a structural system, the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach. Once trained, the machine learning model can accurately predict system responses for a variety of initial conditions and external excitations, with its predictions robustly capturing the responses of different operating configurations, including relocation of the nonlinear region. This work contributes to the advancement of reduced-order modeling techniques and has significant implications for applications in digital twins and structural health monitoring, positing potential future explorations in incorporating additional uncertainties and nonlinear features in the modeling framework."

Key Points

  • The primary objective of the paper is to develop a structure-preserving machine learning framework for predicting the dynamics of systems with isolated nonlinearities.
  • The main method employed is a data-driven machine learning approach using a multilayer perceptron to predict deviatoric forces based on measured boundary responses.
  • A key finding is that the machine learning framework can accurately predict structural dynamics without the direct numerical simulation of nonlinear regions.
  • The significance of the results lies in the framework's ability to accurately model and predict the dynamics of systems with localized nonlinearities efficiently.
  • A novel insight of this work is the use of structure-preserving machine learning to isolate and predict the effects of nonlinearities in dynamic systems.
  • Major limitations include the requirement for measuring responses at the boundary of the nonlinear region and reliance on predefined ideal linear systems.
  • Future research is suggested to focus on developing reduced-order models and ML systems reducing reliance on measurable responses, opening pathways for digital twins and SHM applications.

VpROM: A novel variational autoencoder-boosted reduced order model for the treatment of parametric dependencies in nonlinear systems

Authors: Thomas Simpson, Konstantinos Vlachas, Anthony Garland, Nikolaos Dervilis, Eleni Chatzi

Publication Date: 2024-02-29

File: processed_papers/VpROM a novel variational/VpROM a novel variational.md

Paragraph Summary

"The paper introduces VpROM (Variational autoencoder-boosted reduced order model), a novel approach to creating reduced order models (ROMs) for nonlinear parametric systems that addresses computational inefficiencies associated with conventional methods. Traditional ROM techniques often employ projection-based reduction, such as Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD), which can struggle with nonlinear behaviors and parametric dependencies. VpROM proposes using Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) as a generative modeling tool to derive local reduction bases and dynamically approximate the relationship between parametric inputs and corresponding reduced bases. This new method enhances the flexibility and performance of the ROM by allowing for high-precision representations across diverse response regimes and enabling uncertainty quantification in the model predictions.\n\nThe effectiveness of the proposed VpROM framework is validated through two case studies: a nonlinear two-story shear frame with hysteretic links and a large-scale wind turbine tower featuring plastic behavior. Results demonstrate that VpROM outperforms existing state-of-the-art ROMs, including MACpROM and CpROM, by providing improved accuracy in capturing dynamic responses and reducing computational costs significantly\u2014up to nearly 40 times faster than full-order models (FOMs). The VpROM method allows not only for robust approximations across varying parametric configurations but also for uncertainty assessments on output predictions, making it a promising tool for applications in structural health monitoring and digital twinning."

Key Points

  • The primary research question is how to enhance Reduced Order Models (ROMs) for nonlinear systems with a method that efficiently handles parametric dependencies using machine learning techniques.
  • The main method proposed is the use of a variational autoencoder (VAE) to create a more generalized mapping between parametric inputs and reduced bases for generative modeling.
  • Key findings suggest that using VAEs improves the accuracy and reliability of ROMs in handling complex dependencies and produces more efficient surrogates, leading to better performance compared to traditional interpolation and clustering methods.
  • This work's significance lies in demonstrating that VAEs can improve the robustness and performance of ROMs, offering more reliable predictions under variable system conditions, particularly in complex engineering applications.
  • The novelty of the approach is the integration of VAEs to replace traditional methods in ROMs, thus enabling the treatment of high-dimensional and nonlinear parametric dependencies more effectively.
  • Limitations include the complexity of training the VAE models, which involves tuning numerous hyperparameters, and the extensive effort required compared to traditional ROM methods.
  • Future research could focus on more efficient training processes for VAEs in ROMs, broader validation on various engineering applications, and incorporating real-time capabilities in the models.

Paragraph Summary

"The paper \"Parametric Reduced-Order Modeling For Component-Oriented Treatment And Localized Nonlinear Feature Inclusion\" by Vlachas et al. proposes a new approach to reduced-order modeling (ROM) that combines physics-based reduction with a response decomposition technique specifically designed for systems with localized nonlinearities. This Component-Oriented Reduction (COR) technique applies a \"divide and conquer\" strategy, isolating individual component modes to handle localized nonlinear features while employing global modes to accurately capture the overall dynamics of the system. By using proper orthogonal decomposition (POD) combined with hyper-reduction techniques, the COR-ROM is capable of efficiently approximating the dynamics of complex systems within a broad range of parametric inputs. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of this methodology through numerical examples involving a four-story shear frame and a large-scale kingpin connection, successfully showcasing the approach's capacity to facilitate accelerated computations while maintaining accuracy in representing nonlinear behaviors.\n\nThe authors discuss the limitations of existing modeling techniques when dealing with nonlinear dynamic responses and highlight the robustness and efficiency of the COR-ROM framework. It allows for real-time evaluations relevant to structural health monitoring (SHM) applications, capturing essential physical characteristics and dynamic information on both localized and global scales. The comprehensive results of the numerical tests emphasize that the COR-ROM outperforms traditional global ROM approaches, confirming its utility for complex structural assemblies with localized features. The framework provides a foundation for future advancements in the field, suggesting enhancements that can improve its adaptability to more complex situations with multiple nonlinearities."

Key Points

  • The primary objective of this paper is to propose a reduced-order modeling (ROM) framework combining component-oriented reduction with parametric dependencies to handle localized nonlinear features in dynamic systems.
  • The approach employs physics-based reduction techniques, response decomposition, and proper orthogonal decomposition (POD) to form reduced-order models that capture both global dynamics and localized nonlinearities.
  • Key findings include the ability of the proposed framework to accurately approximate complex system responses while significantly reducing computational costs, as demonstrated in case studies like a four-story shear frame and a kingpin connection with plasticity.
  • The significance lies in the method's efficiency and robustness, offering a balance between computational expense and model accuracy, thus allowing for practical applications in structural health monitoring and digital twins.
  • The novel contribution includes integrating component-oriented reduction with hyper-reduction techniques for capturing nonlinear features, and adaptively sampling parameter spaces using measures like the Modal Assurance Criterion (MAC).
  • Major challenges acknowledged are the assumptions of isolated nonlinear regions and the computational costs involved in the full-order model evaluations required for the training phase.
  • Future research suggestions include extending the framework to automatically identify regions of nonlinearity, addressing cases with zero tangent stiffness better, and exploring further enhancements in sampling strategies to tackle high-dimensional parameter spaces.

Paragraph Summary

"The paper discusses a novel method for enhancing the efficiency of structural dynamics simulations in the presence of localized phenomena, such as cracks or stiffness reductions. Traditional projection-based Model Order Reduction (MOR) techniques face challenges when dealing with varying damage scenarios, often requiring expensive full-order model solutions to adapt to changes. The proposed approach enriches an initial low-dimensional basis derived from a healthy system with selected columns from the flexibility matrix, allowing for effective representation of the system's response under localized damage without needing repeated full-order evaluations. By employing a hierarchical matrix representation (HODLR), the method minimizes storage requirements and computational costs associated with flexibility matrix calculations.\n\nThe authors validate the efficiency and accuracy of their method through a series of numerical examples, demonstrating substantial speedups (up to 150 times) in simulating various structural scenarios involving local damage. The model retains precision in capturing dynamic responses while significantly reducing the computational load usually associated with traditional methods. However, the increased computational demands for matrix generation and size limitations based on problem complexity are highlighted as potential drawbacks. Future work aims to optimize the approach further and apply it to dynamic crack propagation problems, thus extending its versatility in structural health monitoring and predictive maintenance."

Key Points

  • The primary objective is to accelerate structural dynamics simulations involving localised phenomena like cracks using matrix compression and model order reduction techniques.
  • The main method involves enriching low-dimensional spaces of a healthy structure with selected columns of its flexibility matrix, and uses the Hierarchically Off-Diagonal Low Rank (HODLR) representation to efficiently compute these matrices.
  • The key finding is that the proposed method achieves significant computational speedups without compromising the accuracy or flexibility of the full order model, as demonstrated in various structural dynamics examples.
  • The significance of this work lies in reducing computational costs for simulating structural dynamics, making it more feasible to conduct large-scale or repeated evaluations needed in scenarios such as Structural Health Monitoring.
  • One novel contribution is the use of flexibility matrix enrichment in reduced order models to adaptively capture the effects of localized damage, which removes the need for full order model evaluations during the online phase.
  • A major limitation is the high computational effort and memory demand in the offline phase due to the large size and number of matrices, limiting scalability for very large systems.
  • The authors suggest exploring different hierarchical matrix structures and improving online compression strategies as future research directions to enhance scalability and online performance.
